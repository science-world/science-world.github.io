FAQ

How do we know what the sun's magnetic field is like?

Scientists know what the sun's magnetic field is like primarily through the Zeeman effect, a phenomenon where the energy levels of atoms split in a magnetic field, which alters the light they emit. Instruments called magnetographs measure these tiny changes to map the strength and direction of the magnetic field across the sun's surface

What does this matter for humans?

The Sun’s magnetosphere acts as our solar system’s primary shield. It blocks deadly cosmic rays and filters intense solar radiation, which allows life to survive on Earth

How strong is it?

The Sun’s magnetic field varies wildly in strength depending on the location and the 11-year solar cycle. While its average surface field is about 1 Gauss or 100 Micro Teslas, active regions (sunspots) can spike to 4000 Gauss or 0.4 Teslas, and recent research suggests the true magnetic strength of the Sun could be significantly higher than previously estimated

How big is it?

About 17 light hours or 120 astronomical units

How did it first form?

Before the Sun fully formed, it was a collapsing cloud of hydrogen and helium gas. This gas contained tiny, weak "seed" magnetic fields from the interstellar medium. As gravity pulled the cloud together to form the proto-Sun, these existing magnetic field lines were trapped and tightly compressed, drastically increasing their strength

What was the Carrington Event?

The Carrington Event was the most powerful geomagnetic storm ever recorded in human history, occurring on September 1–2, 1859. It was triggered by a massive solar flare and subsequent coronal mass ejection (CME)—a colossal blast of plasma and magnetized particles—erupting from the Sun directly toward Earth.

How would a Carrington Event affect us?

A Carrington-level solar superstorm today would cause catastrophic, global-scale disruptions by overloading electrical power grids, destroying satellites, and crippling internet, GPS, and communication networks. Recovery efforts could last anywhere from months to years
