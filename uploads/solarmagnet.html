Plasma!

What is Plasma?

Plasma is a superheated, ionized gas where electrons have been stripped from their atoms, leaving a mixture of free-moving electrons and positively charged ions. While rare to find naturally on Earth, it makes up over 99% of the visible universe.

Key Characteristics

Unlike regular gas, plasma conducts electricity and is highly responsive to magnetic fields. It is created by adding extreme energy in the form of heat or electricity to a gas until electrons break free from the nucleus.

Plasma Makes Up Our Sun!

The Sun is primarily composed of hydrogen and helium in this plasma state, allowing its vast network of charged particles to constantly generate and interact with magnetic fields.




What does this have to do with electromagnetism?

Thinking of the Sun as a massive ball of flowing, charged plasma makes it easy to understand where its magnetic fields originate.

Convection Currents

Deep within the Sun, plasma is heated to roughly 2,000,000°C. Hot plasma rises towards the surface, cools, and sinks back down. Because this plasma is conductive, these chaotic motions twist and stretch magnetic field lines, acting as a small-scale dynamo within the Sun.

Differential Rotation & The Omega Effect

The Sun rotates faster at the equator (~25 days) than at the poles (~35 days). This differential rotation creates the "Omega Effect," stretching the Sun's north-south (poloidal) magnetic field lines and winding them into strong east-west (toroidal) fields around the solar globe.

Helical Motion & The Alpha Effect

As convective plumes rise, the Coriolis force twists them into a helical motion. This motion rotates the toroidal magnetic fields back into a poloidal orientation known as the “Alpha Effect” which plays a key role in reversing the Sun's magnetic poles every 11 years.



Tachocline

The interface layer between the radiative zone and the convective zone.

Strong, sheared magnetic fields are stored and amplified within the tachocline. Sudden shifts in rotation velocity here provide the ideal environment for the Omega Effect to intensify these toroidal fields before they rise.

Magnetic Buoyancy & Flux Emergence

Magnetic pressure makes the plasma inside magnetic field tubes less dense than its surroundings. These buoyant tubes rise through the convective zone to the surface (photosphere), breaking through to form pairs of sunspots with opposing magnetic polarities.
